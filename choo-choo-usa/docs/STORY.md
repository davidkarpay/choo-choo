# The Story of Choo-Choo USA

## The World

Somewhere in America, nestled between rolling green hills and a wide river, stands the Great Roundhouse -- the biggest, coziest train house you ever did see. It is round like a pie, with a tall brick chimney on top and warm golden light glowing from every window. Inside, the tracks spread out like the spokes of a wheel, and at the end of each spoke sleeps a train.

This is where the trains come home at night.

They back in slowly, their big wheels sighing as they settle onto their berths. The roundhouse keeper -- a cheerful old fellow with a lantern and a big ring of keys -- checks on each one. He tops off their water. He feeds their fireboxes with fresh coal. He wipes the soot from their headlamps and whispers, "Rest well now. Big day tomorrow."

And then the roundhouse goes quiet. Just the creak of cooling metal, the soft hiss of settling steam, and the distant hoot of an owl outside.

---

## The Morning

When the first pink light touches the hills, the roundhouse wakes up.

It starts with a rumble. Deep in the belly of the biggest engine -- a great red locomotive named **Big Thunder** -- the firebox coughs to life. Then another engine stirs. Then another. One by one, the trains wake up, their boilers warming, their smokestacks beginning to puff.

The puffing starts slow. *Pff... pff... pff...*

Then faster. *Pff-pff-pff-pff...*

The roundhouse fills with steam and the smell of hot iron and coal. The turntable in the center of the roundhouse begins to spin, lining up with each spoke, and one by one the trains roll out into the morning air.

They blow their whistles as they leave.

**WHOOOOOOO!** (That is Big Thunder. His whistle is the deepest.)

*Wheee-wheee!* (That is Daisy Belle. She pulls the passenger cars and her whistle is high and cheerful.)

**BWAAAAMP!** (That is the horn of Iron Mike. He is a diesel helper engine and he is very loud about it.)

The trains creep out slowly, their wheels clicking over the switches in the yard. Click-click. Click-click. But once they hit the main line -- oh! Then they pick up speed. Their pistons pump. Their connecting rods blur. Their smoke trails out behind them in long white ribbons against the blue sky.

They are off to work.

---

## The Work

What do trains do? Trains carry the world.

**Big Thunder** pulls a long line of hopper cars filled with coal from the mines of West Virginia all the way to the power plants in Ohio. Without Big Thunder, the lights would go out.

**Daisy Belle** pulls passenger cars with reclining seats and dining cars with white tablecloths. People ride Daisy Belle from New York to Chicago. They eat breakfast while the green hills of Pennsylvania roll past the windows. They read books. They sleep in little fold-down beds. A child presses her nose against the glass and watches the farms go by.

**Iron Mike** helps the big engines up the mountain passes. When the grade gets steep, Iron Mike couples onto the back and pushes with all his might. He is small but he is the strongest engine in the yard, pound for pound.

**Starlight Express** runs at night, pulling refrigerated cars full of fresh food -- California oranges to New York, Florida strawberries to Chicago, Texas beef to everywhere. Her headlamp cuts through the darkness and her whistle echoes across sleeping towns.

**Old Faithful** is the oldest engine in the roundhouse. She has been running the same route for fifty years -- Kansas City to Denver, carrying grain from the great wheat fields of Kansas. Her paint is faded and her whistle is scratchy, but she never misses a run. Not once.

**Copper King** pulls tanker cars of fuel and flatcars stacked with new automobiles fresh from the factory in Detroit. He is long and serious and he does not blow his whistle unless he absolutely has to.

---

## The Cargo

Trains pull cargo for every industry in America:

- **Coal and minerals** from the mines -- in open hopper cars, black and dusty
- **Grain and produce** from the farms -- in covered hoppers and refrigerated boxcars
- **Automobiles** from the factories -- on open-air auto rack cars, shiny and new
- **Fuel** from the refineries -- in round silver tanker cars
- **Lumber** from the forests -- on flatcars, smelling like fresh pine
- **Packages and mail** from the cities -- in sealed container cars, stacked two high
- **Livestock** from the ranches -- in ventilated stock cars (the cows moo as they travel)
- **People** from everywhere to everywhere -- in coach cars, sleeper cars, and dining cars

---

## The Stations

Across the country, wherever the tracks cross a town, there is a station. Some stations are grand -- marble floors and high ceilings and clocks with golden hands. Some stations are small -- just a wooden platform and a bench and a sign with the town's name painted on it.

But every station is a place where stories begin and end.

A grandmother arrives to visit her grandchildren. A young man leaves for his first job in the big city. A family loads their luggage for a vacation out west. A conductor calls "ALL ABOARD!" and the platform empties and the train pulls away, and the people left behind wave until it disappears around the bend.

---

## The Night

At the end of the day, as the sun dips below the horizon and the sky turns from gold to purple to deep, deep blue, the trains come home.

One by one they rumble back into the yard, their headlamps cutting through the dusk. They slow down on the approach -- click... click... click... -- and the turntable lines up to welcome them back to their berths.

The roundhouse keeper is waiting with his lantern. He checks the wheels. He drains the boilers. He banks the fires low so they will be easy to restart in the morning.

The trains settle in. The building creaks and groans as the metal cools. The big doors close.

And through the windows, you can see the soft orange glow of the banked fires, like night-lights in a nursery.

The trains are sleeping.

Tomorrow, they will do it all again.

## Narrative Voice

The narration in the game uses this same warm, descriptive, slightly old-fashioned storytelling voice. It speaks directly to the player ("you") as if reading aloud from a storybook. Key qualities:

- **Wonder.** Everything about trains is presented as marvelous and worthy of attention.
- **Warmth.** The trains are characters. They have names and personalities.
- **Specificity.** Real geography, real cargo, real industries -- not vague hand-waving.
- **Gentleness.** The world is safe and good. Work is meaningful. Night brings rest.
- **Rhythm.** The prose has a read-aloud cadence -- short sentences punctuated by long, rolling ones.

When the player clicks on a train or a station or a cargo car, the narrator speaks in this voice to tell them what they are seeing.
